Diabetic Retinopathy
1 of 48
Microvascular complication of diabetes causing retinal ischemia and leakage - Leading cause of blindness in US adults aged 20–64

Diabetic Retinopathy – Risk Factors & Pathophysiology
2 of 48
Duration of diabetes is most important risk factor - Pericyte loss and basement membrane thickening lead to microaneurysms and capillary nonperfusion

Mild Nonproliferative Diabetic Retinopathy
3 of 48
Microaneurysms only - Earliest clinical stage of diabetic retinopathy

Moderate Nonproliferative Diabetic Retinopathy
4 of 48
More than microaneurysms but less than severe NPDR - No 4-2-1 rule findings

Severe Nonproliferative Diabetic Retinopathy
5 of 48
4-2-1 rule - ≥4 quadrants of hemorrhages, ≥2 quadrants venous beading, or ≥1 quadrant IRMA

Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy
6 of 48
Neovascularization of disc or elsewhere - Risk of vitreous hemorrhage and tractional retinal detachment

High-Risk Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy
7 of 48
NVD >¼ disc diameter or any NVD/NVE with hemorrhage - Requires prompt treatment

Clinically Significant Macular Edema
8 of 48
Retinal thickening within 500 µm of fovea or hard exudates with adjacent thickening - Vision-threatening diabetic macular edema

Diabetic Retinopathy – Management & Follow-Up
9 of 48
Anti-VEGF for DME and PDR - PRP for high-risk PDR with follow-up based on severity

Hypertensive Retinopathy
10 of 48
Retinal vascular changes from systemic hypertension - Classified using Keith-Wagener-Barker system

Keith-Wagener-Barker Grade 1
11 of 48
Generalized arteriolar narrowing - Mild hypertension

Keith-Wagener-Barker Grade 2
12 of 48
AV nicking and focal arteriolar narrowing - Chronic hypertension

Keith-Wagener-Barker Grade 3
13 of 48
Flame hemorrhages, cotton wool spots, and hard exudates - End-organ damage

Keith-Wagener-Barker Grade 4
14 of 48
Optic disc edema - Malignant hypertension

Central Retinal Vein Occlusion
15 of 48
Dilated tortuous veins with diffuse retinal hemorrhages (blood and thunder appearance) - Risk factors include hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia, and glaucoma; complications include macular edema and neovascular glaucoma; manage with anti-VEGF and close monitoring

Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion
16 of 48
Sectoral retinal hemorrhages in a venous distribution - Often superotemporal; treat macular edema with anti-VEGF

Central Retinal Artery Occlusion
17 of 48
Acute retinal ischemia from arterial occlusion - Cherry red spot with retinal whitening; ophthalmic emergency requiring immediate stroke workup

Branch Retinal Artery Occlusion
18 of 48
Sectoral retinal ischemia - Requires systemic vascular evaluation

Central Serous Chorioretinopathy
19 of 48
Serous detachment of neurosensory retina - Associated with corticosteroids, stress, and pregnancy; often self-resolving

Ocular Ischemic Syndrome
20 of 48
Chronic ocular hypoperfusion from severe carotid artery disease - Mid-peripheral hemorrhages and anterior segment ischemia

Valsalva Retinopathy
21 of 48
Pre-retinal or ILM hemorrhage after sudden increase in intrathoracic pressure - Usually self-resolving

Retinopathy of Prematurity
22 of 48
Peripheral retinal ischemia in premature infants exposed to oxygen - Severity based on zone and stage

Radiation Retinopathy
23 of 48
Delayed microvascular retinal damage after radiation therapy - Resembles diabetic retinopathy

Coats Disease
24 of 48
Idiopathic unilateral retinal telangiectasia in young males - Can lead to exudative retinal detachment

Sickle Cell Retinopathy
25 of 48
Retinal ischemia from sickled erythrocytes - Sea-fan neovascularization is characteristic

Retinal Arterial Macroaneurysm
26 of 48
Focal dilation of retinal artery in elderly hypertensive women - Can cause multilayer retinal hemorrhage

Racemose Hemangioma
27 of 48
Congenital arteriovenous malformation of retina and brain - Associated with Wyburn-Mason syndrome

X-Linked Retinoschisis
28 of 48
Congenital splitting of retinal layers in males - Stellate foveal pattern on OCT

Acquired Retinoschisis
29 of 48
Peripheral retinal splitting in older adults - Typically asymptomatic and nonprogressive

Pathologic Myopia
30 of 48
High myopia with degenerative fundus changes - Increased risk of retinal tears and CNVM

Angioid Streaks
31 of 48
Breaks in Bruch membrane - Associated with pseudoxanthoma elasticum and sickle cell disease

Retinitis Pigmentosa
32 of 48
Inherited rod-cone dystrophy - Presents with nyctalopia and peripheral vision loss

Stargardt Disease
33 of 48
Autosomal recessive macular dystrophy - Beaten bronze fundus and silent choroid

Best Disease
34 of 48
Autosomal dominant vitelliform dystrophy - Abnormal EOG is diagnostic

Familial Exudative Vitreoretinopathy
35 of 48
Inherited disorder with peripheral avascular retina - Mimics retinopathy of prematurity in full-term infants

Leber Congenital Amaurosis
36 of 48
Severe congenital retinal dystrophy presenting in infancy - Common cause of childhood blindness

Acute Retinal Necrosis
37 of 48
Herpetic necrotizing retinitis - Requires immediate systemic antiviral therapy

Toxoplasmosis
38 of 48
Protozoal retinochoroiditis with headlight-in-the-fog appearance - Treat active lesions only

Primary Intraocular Lymphoma
39 of 48
Masquerade syndrome often associated with CNS lymphoma - Poor prognosis

Posterior Scleritis
40 of 48
Inflammation of posterior sclera causing pain and vision loss - T-sign on B-scan ultrasound

Retinoblastoma
41 of 48
Most common intraocular malignancy in children - Leukocoria is classic presenting sign

Congenital Hypertrophy of the RPE
42 of 48
Flat pigmented retinal lesion present at birth - Bear tracks are benign

Macular Telangiectasia
43 of 48
Idiopathic perifoveal capillary dilation - Type 2 is most common

Macular Hole
44 of 48
Full-thickness defect of the macula - Treated with vitrectomy if symptomatic

Cystoid Macular Edema
45 of 48
Intraretinal cystic spaces from vascular leakage - Treat underlying cause

Dry Age-Related Macular Degeneration
46 of 48
Drusen and RPE atrophy - AREDS recommended for intermediate disease

Wet Age-Related Macular Degeneration
47 of 48
Choroidal neovascular membrane formation - Requires urgent anti-VEGF therapy

Choroidal Melanoma
48 of 48
Most common primary intraocular malignancy in adults - Management depends on size and risk factors
