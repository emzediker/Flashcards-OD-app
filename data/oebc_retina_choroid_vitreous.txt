Diabetic Retinopathy
1 of 52
Leading cause of blindness in US adults age 20–64 - Risk factors include duration of diabetes (most important), poor glycemic control, and large glucose fluctuations

Mild Nonproliferative Diabetic Retinopathy
2 of 52
Microaneurysms only - Earliest stage of diabetic retinopathy

Moderate Nonproliferative Diabetic Retinopathy
3 of 52
More than microaneurysms but less than severe NPDR - Intermediate stage without 4-2-1 findings

Severe Nonproliferative Diabetic Retinopathy
4 of 52
4-2-1 rule - ≥4 quadrants of hemorrhages, ≥2 quadrants of venous beading, or ≥1 quadrant of IRMA

Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy
5 of 52
Neovascularization present - May include NVD or NVE

High-Risk Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy
6 of 52
NVD >¼ disc diameter or any NVD/NVE with vitreous or preretinal hemorrhage - Requires prompt treatment

Clinically Significant Macular Edema
7 of 52
Retinal thickening within 500 µm of fovea or hard exudates with adjacent thickening - Vision-threatening DME

Diabetic Retinopathy Treatment
8 of 52
Depends on stage - Anti-VEGF for DME and PDR, PRP for high-risk PDR

Diabetic Retinopathy Follow-Up
9 of 52
Ranges from annual to monthly - Frequency increases with disease severity

Gestational Diabetes Retinopathy
10 of 52
Same findings as diabetic retinopathy - Followed more frequently during pregnancy

Hypertensive Retinopathy
11 of 52
Retinal vascular changes from hypertension - Classified using Keith-Wagener-Barker system

Keith-Wagener-Barker Group 1
12 of 52
Generalized arteriolar narrowing - Mild hypertension

Keith-Wagener-Barker Group 2
13 of 52
AV nicking - Chronic hypertension

Keith-Wagener-Barker Group 3
14 of 52
Flame hemorrhages, cotton wool spots, hard exudates - End-organ damage

Keith-Wagener-Barker Group 4
15 of 52
Optic disc edema - Malignant hypertension

Presumed Ocular Histoplasmosis Syndrome
16 of 52
Fungal infection associated with bird or bat droppings - Diagnosed with 2 of the triad findings

Valsalva Retinopathy
17 of 52
Pre-retinal or ILM hemorrhage after sudden Valsalva maneuver - Usually self-resolving

Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion
18 of 52
Most common retinal vein occlusion - Often superotemporal

Central Retinal Vein Occlusion
19 of 52
Dilated tortuous veins and diffuse hemorrhages - Risk of neovascular glaucoma

Branch Retinal Artery Occlusion
20 of 52
Sectoral retinal ischemia - Requires systemic and neurologic workup

Central Retinal Artery Occlusion
21 of 52
Ophthalmic emergency - Refer immediately to stroke center

Central Serous Chorioretinopathy
22 of 52
Serous macular detachment in type A individuals - Associated with steroids and pregnancy

Ocular Ischemic Syndrome
23 of 52
Severe carotid artery disease causing ocular hypoperfusion - Associated with mid-peripheral hemorrhages

Retinopathy of Prematurity
24 of 52
Peripheral retinal ischemia from excess oxygen exposure - Seen in premature infants

Radiation Retinopathy
25 of 52
Delayed retinal ischemia after radiation exposure - Resembles diabetic retinopathy

Coats Disease
26 of 52
Unilateral retinal telangiectasia in young males - Can lead to exudative retinal detachment

Sickle Cell Retinopathy
27 of 52
Retinal ischemia from sickled erythrocytes - Sea fan neovascularization is characteristic

Retinal Arterial Macroaneurysm
28 of 52
Focal dilation of retinal artery in elderly hypertensive women - May cause multilayer hemorrhage

Racemose Hemangioma
29 of 52
Congenital AV malformation of retina and brain - Associated with Wyburn-Mason syndrome

X-Linked Retinoschisis
30 of 52
Congenital splitting of retinal layers - Stellate foveal pattern on OCT

Acquired Retinoschisis
31 of 52
Peripheral retinal splitting in older adults - Usually asymptomatic

Pathologic Myopia
32 of 52
High myopia with degenerative fundus changes - Risk of retinal tears and CNVM

Angioid Streaks
33 of 52
Breaks in Bruch membrane associated with PXE - Risk of choroidal rupture

Retinitis Pigmentosa
34 of 52
Inherited rod-cone dystrophy - Presents with nyctalopia and tunnel vision

Stargardt Disease
35 of 52
Autosomal recessive macular dystrophy - Beaten bronze appearance and silent choroid

Best Disease
36 of 52
Autosomal dominant vitelliform dystrophy - Abnormal EOG is diagnostic

Familial Exudative Vitreoretinopathy
37 of 52
ROP-like disease in full-term infants - Peripheral avascular retina

Leber Congenital Amaurosis
38 of 52
Severe congenital retinal dystrophy - Most common cause of childhood blindness

Acute Retinal Necrosis
39 of 52
Herpetic necrotizing retinitis - Requires immediate antiviral therapy

Toxoplasmosis
40 of 52
Protozoal retinitis with headlight-in-the-fog appearance - Treat active lesions only

Primary Intraocular Lymphoma
41 of 52
Masquerade syndrome often from CNS lymphoma - Poor prognosis

Posterior Scleritis
42 of 52
Painful inflammatory condition of posterior sclera - T-sign on B-scan

Retinoblastoma
43 of 52
Most common intraocular malignancy in children - Leukocoria is classic sign

Congenital Hypertrophy of the RPE
44 of 52
Flat pigmented lesion present at birth - Bear tracks are benign

Macular Telangiectasia
45 of 52
Idiopathic perifoveal capillary dilation - Type 2 most common

Macular Hole
46 of 52
Full-thickness defect of the macula - Treated with vitrectomy if symptomatic

Cystoid Macular Edema
47 of 52
Intraretinal cystic spaces from vascular leakage - Treat underlying cause

Dry Age-Related Macular Degeneration
48 of 52
Drusen and RPE atrophy - AREDS indicated for intermediate disease

Wet Age-Related Macular Degeneration
49 of 52
Choroidal neovascular membrane present - Treat with anti-VEGF urgently

Epiretinal Membrane
50 of 52
Fibrocellular membrane on inner retina - Surgery if vision significantly reduced

Choroidal Melanoma
51 of 52
Most common primary intraocular malignancy in adults - Monitor or refer based on risk factors

Birdshot Chorioretinopathy
52 of 52
HLA-A29 associated posterior uveitis - Requires long-term immunosuppression
