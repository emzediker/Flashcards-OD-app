Albinism
1 of  Forty-Nine
Types: Ocular albinism (X-linked recessive; decreased number of melanosomes in eyes with normal pigment per melanosome); Oculocutaneous albinism (autosomal recessive; decreased melanin in all melanosomes of skin and eyes).
Symptoms: Blur and photophobia.
Ocular Findings: High refractive error, nystagmus, strabismus, reduced stereoacuity, transillumination defects, hypopigmented fundus, foveal hypoplasia.

Alcoholism
2 of 49
Systemic Associations: Renal disease, hepatitis, thiamine (B1) deficiency.
Labs: Decreased ALT and B1 levels.
Wernicke’s Encephalopathy: Ataxia, ophthalmoplegia, confusion.
Korsakoff Syndrome: Irreversible B1 deficiency causing memory loss, amnesia, apathy, poor communication, loss of intuition.
Ocular Findings: Toxic optic neuropathy, downbeat nystagmus, demyelination.

Ankylosing Spondylitis
3 of 49
Associations: HLA-B27 positive.
Ocular Findings: Chronic or recurrent uveitis.
Systemic Clues: Chronic lower back pain.
Workup: Spinal X-ray warranted.

Rheumatoid Arthritis
4 of 49
Ocular Associations: Most common cause of scleritis including scleromalacia perforans (necrotizing without inflammation); dry eye; ulcerative keratitis.
Labs: Positive RF.
Management: Systemic NSAIDs, systemic steroids, immunosuppressive therapy.

Juvenile Rheumatoid Arthritis
5 of 49
Ocular Associations: Most common cause of recurrent non-granulomatous anterior uveitis in children.
Labs: Positive ANA, negative RF.

Asthma
6 of 49
Clinical Pearl: Avoid prescribing beta-blockers due to bronchoconstriction risk.

Eczema / Atopic Dermatitis
7 of 49

Behçet Disease
8 of 49
Epidemiology: Asians or Mediterraneans; HLA-B5 or HLA-B12.
Systemic Findings: Oral ulcers, genital ulcers.
Ocular Findings: Bilateral non-granulomatous uveitis with hypopyon; late macular edema, disc edema, ischemic optic neuritis.
Treatment: Topical steroids for uveitis; sub-Tenon’s steroids for posterior involvement.

Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus
17 of 49
Epithelial Keratitis: SPK progressing to pseudodendrites.
Stromal Keratitis: Anterior stromal infiltrates, disciform keratitis, peripheral edema; chronic scarring possible.
Iritis: AC reaction, TIDs, keratic precipitates.
Treatment: Famciclovir 500 mg TID x 7 days; Acyclovir 800 mg 5x/day x 7 days; Valacyclovir 1000 mg TID x 7 days.
Steroids: Only for stromal keratitis or endotheliitis when no active epithelial disease.
Complication: Post-herpetic neuralgia; may require long-term antiviral prophylaxis.


Herpes Simplex Virus
18 of 49
Epidemiology: #1 cause of infectious blindness in the US; HSV-1.
Epithelial Disease: Dendrites with terminal bulbs; edge staining with rose bengal.
Stromal Keratitis: Immune keratitis without epithelial defect.
Necrotizing Keratitis: Stromal inflammation with epithelial defect; high risk of corneal melt.
Endotheliitis: Disciform keratitis; often with granulomatous uveitis.
Treatment: Zirgan 5x/day or Viroptic 9x/day; oral antivirals at half HZO dose; topical steroids only if no epithelial disease.

Homocystinuria
19 of 49

Ocular Finding: Ectopia lentis.

HIV / AIDS
20 of 49
Most Common Opportunistic Retinal Infection: CMV retinitis.
Presentation: Unilateral or bilateral; often asymptomatic; floaters possible.
Treatment: IV and intravitreal antivirals.

Progressive Outer Retinal Necrosis
21 of 49
Etiology: HSV or VZV in HIV patients.
Course: Anterior uveitis followed by yellow retinal infiltrates with rapid progression.
Treatment: Aggressive antiviral therapy.

Syphilis
43 of 49
Etiology: Treponema pallidum.
Primary: Chancre 4–12 weeks after infection.
Secondary: 4–10 weeks after primary; maculopapular rash on palms, soles, trunk.
Anterior Segment: Any uveitis (granulomatous common), scleritis, episcleritis, keratitis.
Posterior Segment: Vitritis, necrotizing retinitis, chorioretinitis, neuroretinitis, papillitis, optic neuritis; ground-glass retinitis.
Congenital: Peg teeth, interstitial keratitis, deafness.
Testing: RPR, FTA-ABS.
Treatment: IV penicillin G followed by IM penicillin G.
