Papilledema
Papilledema
1 of 30
Definition: Optic disc swelling due to increased intracranial pressure (↑ICP).
Symptoms: Transient bilateral vision loss after rising, headache, diplopia, vomiting.
Signs: Bilateral swollen discs with blurred margins, possible peripapillary hemorrhages, loss of SVP, enlarged blind spot.
Workup: Neuroimaging (MRI/CT) to rule out mass → lumbar puncture with opening pressure.
Clinical Thresholds: CSF opening pressure >200 mm H2O (non-obese) or >250 mm H2O (obese).
Treatment: Treat underlying cause.
Follow-Up: Based on underlying etiology.
Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)
Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)
2 of 30
Definition: Idiopathic elevation of CSF pressure, classically in overweight women of childbearing age.
Treatment: Weight loss; acetazolamide 250 mg QID (titrate); short course steroids; consider surgical shunt if needed.
Follow-Up: ~3 weeks if symptomatic; ~3 months if stable.
Key Point: Chronic papilledema can lead to permanent VF loss and decreased acuity.
Arteritic Anterior Ischemic Optic Neuropathy (AAION)
Arteritic Anterior Ischemic Optic Neuropathy (AAION)
3 of 30
Definition: Arteritic AION most commonly due to giant cell arteritis.
Symptoms: Sudden painless vision loss (>55), headache, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, PMR symptoms.
Signs: RAPD, very poor VA, pale swollen disc, flame hemorrhages, tender/nonpulsatile temporal artery.
Workup: ESR/CRP and temporal artery biopsy.
Treatment: Immediate high-dose steroids.
Steroid Regimen: IV methylprednisolone 250 mg q6h ×12 → oral prednisone 100 mg daily.
Follow-Up: Daily until controlled, then monthly.
Non-Arteritic Anterior Ischemic Optic Neuropathy (NAION)
Non-Arteritic Anterior Ischemic Optic Neuropathy (NAION)
4 of 30
Definition: Ischemic optic neuropathy associated with vascular risk factors and disc-at-risk.
Risk Factors: Diabetes, HTN, sleep apnea, hyperlipidemia; labile HTN in younger patients.
Signs: RAPD, segmental disc edema, flame hemorrhages, normal ESR/CRP, altitudinal VF loss.
Treatment: Observation and risk-factor modification; avoid nocturnal BP meds.
Follow-Up: ~1 month.
Mnemonic: VIVA – vardenafil, Imitrex, Viagra, amiodarone.
Optic Neuritis
Optic Neuritis
5 of 30
Definition: Inflammatory optic neuropathy; retrobulbar or papillitis.
Symptoms: Pain with eye movement, dyschromatopsia, unilateral vision loss, Uhthoff phenomenon.
Association: ~50% develop MS within 5 years.
Workup: MRI brain/orbits with T2/FLAIR.
Treatment: IV steroids followed by oral steroids.
Visual Field: Central, cecocentral, or arcuate scotomas.
Follow-Up: 4 weeks; if MS q3 months; if no MS MRI q6 months.
Toxic Optic Neuropathy
Toxic Optic Neuropathy
6 of 30
Definition: Progressive bilateral optic neuropathy due to toxins or nutritional deficiency.
Causative Agents: Ethambutol, isoniazid, chloramphenicol, streptomycin, digitalis, lead.
Signs: Bilateral cecocentral scotomas, dyschromatopsia, temporal pallor.
Treatment: Stop offending agent; thiamine, folate/B6, multivitamin.
Follow-Up: ~1 month.
Dominant Optic Atrophy (DOA)
Dominant Optic Atrophy (DOA)
7 of 30
Definition: Hereditary optic neuropathy with childhood onset and strong family history.
Signs: Temporal disc pallor, reduced VA.
Management: No curative treatment; monitor, low vision aids, genetic counseling.
Leber Hereditary Optic Neuropathy (LHON)
Leber Hereditary Optic Neuropathy (LHON)
8 of 30
Definition: Mitochondrial optic neuropathy with onset age 20–40.
Signs: Hyperemic disc, peripapillary telangiectasia without leakage, peripapillary atrophy.
Management: Low vision aids and genetic counseling.
Optic Nerve Hypoplasia
Optic Nerve Hypoplasia
9 of 30
Definition: Congenital small optic disc, often with double-ring sign.
Associations: Maternal diabetes, quinine abuse, alcoholism, LSD.
Management: No treatment; monitor annually.
Optic Nerve Drusen
Optic Nerve Drusen
10 of 30
Definition: Calcified optic nerve deposits mimicking disc edema.
Diagnosis: B-scan ultrasound or MRI.
Management: No treatment; monitor annually.
Optic Nerve Pit
Optic Nerve Pit
11 of 30
Definition: Congenital defect from incomplete closure of embryonic fissure.
Signs: Gray temporal depression ~1/3 DD with peripapillary atrophy.
Complications: Serous macular detachment or retinoschisis.
Treatment: Observe unless SRD; consider vitrectomy + gas.
Follow-Up: Retina referral if SRD; recheck ~1 month.
Optic Nerve Glioma
Optic Nerve Glioma
12 of 30
Definition: Intrinsic optic nerve neoplasm causing progressive vision loss.
Signs: Proptosis, RAPD, disc edema, enlarged optic nerve on CT.
Types: Juvenile pilocytic astrocytoma (children); glioblastoma multiforme (rare adults).
Management: Monitor if stable; surgery if progressive.
Follow-Up: ~1 month.
Optic Disc Melanocytoma
Optic Disc Melanocytoma
13 of 30
Definition: Benign pigmented tumor near optic disc.
Signs: Jet-black lesion with fuzzy borders adjacent to disc.
Follow-Up: Annual OCT, fundus photos, OCT-A.
Optic Nerve Sheath Meningioma
Optic Nerve Sheath Meningioma
14 of 30
Definition: Tumor of optic nerve meninges, classically in middle-aged women.
Signs: Progressive painless vision loss, color vision defects, optociliary shunt vessels.
Management: Manage IOP; radiation/chemo/surgery if malignant.
Follow-Up: Neuroimaging, color vision, VF q6 months.
Pituitary Adenoma
Pituitary Adenoma
15 of 30
Definition: Sellar tumor compressing optic chiasm.
Signs: Bitemporal hemianopia, optic atrophy, RAPD, endocrine dysfunction.
Management: Neurosurgical referral; steroids if pituitary apoplexy.
Follow-Up: Post-op per neurosurgery.
Cortical Blindness
Cortical Blindness
16 of 30
Definition: Bilateral occipital lobe dysfunction.
Signs: Preserved pupillary reflexes, Riddoch phenomenon, Anton syndrome.
Symptoms: Sudden complete vision loss, visual hallucinations.
Management: Treat underlying cause; rehabilitation.
Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency
Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency
17 of 30
Definition: Posterior circulation TIA.
Symptoms: Flashes, amaurosis fugax, diplopia, vertigo, nystagmus, syncope, homonymous hemianopia.
Management: Treat vascular risk factors; PCP/cardiology follow-up.
Migraine
Migraine
18 of 30
Definition: Recurrent headache disorder lasting 4–72 hours.
Triggers: Stress, sleep deprivation, alcohol, cheese, nitrates, chocolate, hormones, weather, OCs.
Types: Common (no aura), Classic (aura), Ocular migraine (aura only).
Treatment: NSAIDs, anti-nausea meds, trigger journal.
Follow-Up: 4–6 weeks.
Cluster Headache
Cluster Headache
19 of 30
Definition: Severe unilateral headache lasting 10–120 minutes in clusters.
Signs: Conjunctival injection, flushing, possible Horner syndrome.
Treatment: High-flow O2, pain control; MRI to rule out tumor.
Sinus Headache
Sinus Headache
20 of 30
Definition: Facial pain localized to affected sinus.
Treatment: Decongestants, allergy meds, saline rinse, NSAIDs/Tylenol.
Follow-Up: ~10 days.
Argyll Robertson Pupil
Argyll Robertson Pupil
21 of 30
Definition: Near-light dissociation due to neurosyphilis.
Signs: Small pupils that accommodate but poorly react to light.
Workup/Treatment: VDRL, FTA-ABS; IV penicillin G.
Follow-Up: Annual eye exam.
Horner Syndrome
Horner Syndrome
22 of 30
Definition: Sympathetic pathway lesion.
Signs: Ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis.
Localization: 1st order stroke; 2nd order Pancoast tumor; 3rd order vascular.
Workup: Pharmacologic testing and systemic evaluation.
Adie Tonic Pupil
Adie Tonic Pupil
23 of 30
Definition: Acute dilated pupil from parasympathetic dysfunction.
Symptoms: Near blur, photophobia.
Diagnosis: Dilute pilocarpine response.
Treatment: Observation; pilocarpine 0.125% BID–QID.
Follow-Up: ~1 month.
